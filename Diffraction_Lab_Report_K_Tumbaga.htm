Introduction:

            Diffraction is defined as the bending of waves around obstacles in their path (Diffraction). Parts of the light waves interact with one another and this creates patterns of dark and light. Diffraction is dependent on the size of the obstacle that the wave of light is trying to bend around and because the wavelength of light is so small, the obstacle being used must also be relatively small (Diffraction of Light). The light areas formed from diffraction are areas with an increased intensity which shows constructive interference whereas the dark areas have a decreased intensity of light showing destructive interference. “As the waves spread, they tend to cancel out or reinforce each other in different directions, thus causing the characteristic rippled patterns of waves” (Funk & Wagnalls). These rippled patterns are seen throughout this experiment and each ripple is what is considered to be a fringe.

            In this experiment, a piece of hair is taped across the output portion of a laser and the light from the laser is diffracted around the hair creating a pattern of light and dark spots on a screen. The spots are named based on their position relative to the center of the pattern created. The center of the pattern is called the zero-order fringe and is labeled as m=0. M is the order of the fringe and going outward from the zero-order fringe in the ripple pattern on both sides would be the first order fringe which is labeled as m=1, then the next fringe is the second order fringe and is labeled m=2 and this continues for each fringe.

            By measuring the distance between each of the fringes the diameter of the hair will be calculated using formula 1. In formula 1, d is the diameter of the hair, m is the fringe order, λ is the wavelength of light, x is the distance from the hair to the screen and y is the distance from the center fringe to the fringe being measured. Goal:

            The goal of this experiment is to use diffraction to measure the diameter of a hair. By using diffraction to measure the diameter of the hair, a better understanding of how light bends around an object is obtained as well as a better understanding of destructive and constructive interference.

Experimental:

A piece of hair was taped across the aperture of a laser pointer, and the laser pointer was supported by two clothespins. The laser pointer was pointed at a wall 3 meters away from where the paper was taped to serve as a screen. The laser was turned on and the pattern on the screen was observed. The center of the pattern was labeled and marked as “m=0” and the positions on either side of the center were marked as well. The distance from the laser pointer to the wall was measured and recorded as being 300cm.The distance between the central fringe (m=0) and each of the fringes was recorded. The wavelength of the laser was recorded as 650 nm. Formula 1 was used to calculate the diameter of the hair depending on how far the fringe in question was from the central fringe.

 Discussion:

            In table 1 the diameter of brown hair was calculated, and the results were recorded for the diameter of the hair dependent on the distance that the fringe was from the zero-order fringe. The results were then converted from nm to μm using the conversion factor 1 μm= 1000 nm. An average of all the calculated diameters was taken and found to be 85.62 μm.

When comparing the average diameters of brown hair and blonde hair from table 2, the brown hair produces a larger average diameter than the blonde hair which means that brown hair is typically thicker than blonde hair. Some students in the class, however, had red hair and red hair based on the values in table 2 appears to be thinner than brown or blonde hair. Based on these conclusions, a practical use of diffusion would be to identify the hair color of a victim based on the diameter of a single hair. The diameter of the hair can also be compared in terms of ethnic background which would also provide forensic scientists with information pertaining to a crime victim when the diameter of their hair was measured.

            One source of possible error in this experiment is imprecision when taking measurements. Various groups compared their average data for the diameter of the hair they tested, however, every group may not have measured the distance from m=0 to each fringe mark the same way leading to skewed results.
